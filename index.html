Life as an author

Ruskin Bond is a renowned contemporary Indian author of British descent. Born in 1934, he published his first book ‘The Room on The Roof’ at the age of seventeen for which he was awarded the John Llewellyn Rhys Prize in 1957. Beyond this, Bond has written over 500 short stories, essays, and novellas. Further, he has written more than 40 books for children. It suffices to say that through his avid writing career, Bond has had a sizable impact on Indian literature. He received the Sahitya Akademi Award for English writing in India in 1992, the Padma Shri in 1999, the Delhi government’s Lifetime Achievement Award in 2012 and Padma Bhushan in 2014 for his contribution to literature1. Many of his books are based in or around the towns of Dehradun and Mussoorie at the foothills of the himalayas.

Books

Ruskin Bond’s first book, ‘The Room on The roof’ which was followed by the book ‘Vagrants in The Valley’ are coming-of-age stories which follow the 17 year-old protagonist, Rusty in his adventures across the city of Dehradun. Like a quintessential book centered around an adolescent protagonist, the book deals with multiple literary themes such as isolation, loneliness, self-doubt, and identity crisis.
As the author weaves for us this Dehradun that Rusty and his friend live in, he very vividly describes multiple aspects of Dehradun that show the readers how the town of Dehradun organically developed into a city in post-colonial India. Through its bazaars, hills, maidaans, and street vendors, Ruskin tells the story of an ambitious India that is ready to leave behind its colonial past and all the more ready to embrace an uncertain future that is full of opportunities.

Ruskin writes about his childhood in Dehradun

“Formally, it’s known as Dehradun, but in the 1940s and 1950s, when we were young, everyone called it Dehra.
That’s where I spent much of my childhood, boyhood, and early manhood, and it was the Dehra I wrote about in many of my books and stories.
It was very different from the Dehradun of today—much smaller, much greener, considerably less crowded; sleepier too, and somewhat laid-back, easy-going; fond of gossip, but tolerant of human foibles. A place of bicycles and pony-drawn tongas. Only a few cars; no three-wheelers. And you could walk almost anywhere, at any time of the year, night or day.”
(Excerpt from the introduction to 'A Town Called Dehra')

A trip through Dehra along it's landmarks

  • The Bazaar
  • In the past
  • Today

Paltan Bazar in Dehradun is a vibrant and the biggest market that is located in the heart of this beautiful city. It is located near the Rajpur road and stretches from the railway station to the clock Tower. Paltan Bazaar plays a very significant role in bringing commercialisation to Dehradun. Since 1935. Paltan Bazaar was the first commercial market of Dehradun. Paltan Bazaar is a collaboration of people from many cultural backgrounds who came from Pakistan. the majority being the Banwal or the Bannu community of Dehradun.

Today, the Bazaar continues to remain a tourist attraction and serves as a central landmark to the city. It is a popular destination for those interested in shopping and tasting local delicacies.

  • The Clocktower
  • In the past
  • Today

The clocktower as Ruskin elaborates, had been built from public subscriptions but not enough money had been gathered for the addition of a clock.

Today, the Clocktower continues to be a central landmark to the city, situated near the biggest bazaar or economic centre i.e Paltan Bazaar, and the railway station. It is surrounded by multiple banks and offices. The central government’s holiday homes are also situated near the Clocktower.

  • The Canal
  • In the past
  • Today

The canals were once elemental in the production of Dehradun’s Basmati rice. So much so that Dehradun was once referred to as the city of canals.

By the year 2007, all of the canals in the city of Dehradun were covered up despite the case made for ther ecological benefits of these canals. The canals were closed up as an attempt at improving the traffic flow through the city once it was turned into the state’s capital.

  • Cinemas
  • Today

Following a similar pattern as the rest of the country, Dehradun has seen single-screen cinemas shut down with time due to stiff competition from multiplexes. As of a 2015 report10, only 5 single-screen cinemas including Orient cinemas which Ruskin mentions in his book, are still open in the city.

  • Green's Hotel during World War 2
  • In the past
  • Today

During the Second World War, Dehradun’s central internment camp was used to house captured Axis troops11. There had always been an elaborate allied military presence in Dehradun facilitated by the railway routes leading to Dehradun. The increased military presence provided a substantial temporary stimulus to the local economy.

Today, Dehradun has a category I cantonment12. As of the 2001 census, the cantonment has a population of around sixty thousand.

  • Churches
  • In the past
  • Today

Dehradun has multiple churches that are as much as a hundred years old including St. Thomas’ church and St Paul’s church which Ruskin briefly mentions in multiple chapters across his books.

Many of these churches are still extremely well maintained and are rallied around by Christian compatriots13. These churches are not only extremely astounding but also have a rich history and heritage.

A Cartographic Analysis of Dehradun and Ruskin Bond's books

Methodology

  • Read through the selected books, and isolate toponyms and other useful information that can be used in further steps.
  • Once we have obtained the toponyms, we must study, sort, and tag them based on multiple factors such as relation between textual space and geospace, modus of projected spaces and function of literary space among others.
  • Using toponyms obtained and sorted in the previous step, we begin the process of geocoding. We could do this process separately for all of the available categories of tags as obtained.
  • Finally, we use the maps obtained after geocoding to analyze and contextualize Bond’s work and study the changes that have manifested in the city of Dehradun.
  • Produce all the results obtained from the project on a public website and use them to tell the story of Ruskin Bond and a changing Dehradun.
